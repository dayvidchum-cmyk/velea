The astrology — how Velea reads a chart
1. Which house are “parents”? Two parts of the engine disagree. One reads parents as the 4th and 9th houses (mother and father — this is what the textbook says). The other sends the paid “Parents & Roots” reading to the 12th house with the lunar nodes as ancestry. YOUR RULING (20 July): both are correct and both stay. The 4th and 9th are specifically mother and father; ancestry is roots — what your parents inherited and passed to you. They answer different questions, so neither collapses into the other. DONE: the defect was never that one was wrong — it was that both claimed both words (“Parents / roots” on the 4th+9th, “Parents & Roots” on the 12th), which let the reading fold them into one theme. The convergence engine now says “Parents — mother and father”, and the lens area now leads with ancestry and roots — what your parents themselves inherited and handed on. A test keeps them apart.

One small thing left, and it is yours because it is visible: the chip a user taps still reads “Parents & Roots” on an area that is now explicitly about ancestry. “Roots & Ancestry” would match your ruling. I did not rename a chip you look at without asking.

Then you sent the whole map — 2nd (Kula, the family line), 4th (mother), 5th (Purva Punya, what you carry forward), 8th (genetic inheritance, ancestral trauma, estates), 9th (father as first teacher), 10th (his standing), 12th (the departed, Pitri Dosha) — plus the karakas: Moon the mother, Sun the father, Ketu the ancestral root. It is captured verbatim in canon/lineage-doctrine.md. I stopped implementing when I saw it was still arriving, because building house 2 while house 8 was still coming is how a method gets half-applied. The engine reads two of those eight. I asked whether ancestry is one chip and one lens, or a spread across seven houses and three planets the reading draws on wherever it is lit. YOUR RULING (20 July): the spread. BUILT — in one pass, and it reaches the reading. All seven strands with your karakas and the question each carries: the family line, the mother, what you carry forward, what came down through the blood, the father, his standing over you, the departed. A strand lights only when a running period-lord or the year lord is genuinely tied to it, or a slow transit lands on it — fast movers are excluded on purpose, or the Moon would find ancestral trauma every third day. The reading is told to fold a lit strand into the day, never as a header, never naming a house number, and never to reach for the ancestors when nothing is lit.

What it deliberately does NOT do: score or rank the strands. How loud one should be is a method call you have not made, and inventing one is the unowned weighting this run has been removing.
Still open: the 12th-house lens wording does not yet say Pitrus / Moksha / Pitri Dosha, and the chip still reads “Parents & Roots”.
2. Which nakshatra table wins? There are six lists in the app classifying the 27 stars, and only one has a source citation. On four stars — Magha, Purva Phalguni, Purva Ashadha and Vishakha — the cited book calls them fierce or mixed while the mode engine treats them as expansive. YOUR RULING (20 July): the cited tables win. (And you are right to balk — “fierce” is not “expansive”.) DONE. Magha, Purva Phalguni and Purva Ashadha now read as containment; Vishakha reads as no-shift. The replacement values come from what the canon says each nature supports and avoids — fierce is “force, demolition, ruthlessness… avoid beginnings and journeys”; mixed is “avoid the extremes, neither launch nor cut” — not from my taste. This touches 57 days a year (15.6%).

One thing I deliberately did NOT do, and it is a question back to you. Deriving every star’s score from its nature would have moved eleven stars, not four. The other seven — Hasta, Punarvasu, Shravana, Shatabhisha and the three Uttaras — are currently “no shift” against a canon nature of swift, movable or fixed. That is not a contradiction, it is just saying less, so changing it would be me rewriting your method under cover of your ruling. Should the cited nature DRIVE every score, or only overrule the ones that contradict it? They are left alone until you say, and a test stops anyone finishing the job by accident.
3. When does a day “become” its star? — ANSWERED, and it was neither of my two options. I offered you “sunrise or majority”. You gave a third answer, and it is the one the book actually describes: the day is NAMED for the blueprint at sunrise, and the READING is two-part. On a transition day an honest reading says “favourable for creative work until 3:00 PM, after which the focus shifts” — it names the hour. Blending the day into whichever star holds the majority is what the generic apps do, and it is why their horoscopes feel off in the morning or the evening.

Velea was doing the thing you described as wrong. The model received only the majority star. It also got a “the day turns at…” sentence — but that is emitted only when the crossing changes the mode, so on every transition day where the mode holds, the model never learned the star turned at all.

DONE: both stars and the clock time now travel to the model on every turning day (starTurn: { fromStar, toStar, atLocalTime, rulesMostOfDay }). ALSO DONE: the prompt now carries the law — name the hour, first star until then, second after, and never flatten to the majority. STILL NOT DONE: the day is named by the majority star rather than the sunrise one. That one changes every reading title on 47% of days, and I am not flipping it without you seeing it.
4. Which cancelled-fall rules are Velea’s? You asked what the book says and I wrote it out verse by verse. A third has no classical verse behind it at all — it is a modern author’s reading. Nothing was changed on the strength of that.
5. Which yogas should the reader actually hear? The engine detects far more combinations than any reading mentions. There is no rule for which ones are worth a person’s attention, so the choice is currently made by whatever the model finds interesting.
